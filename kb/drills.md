# Drill Inventory

**Tags:** `drills`, `practice`, `standstill`, `hershyzer`, `towel`, `one_step`

Drills are ordered from most-foundational to most-complex. Master each
before layering the next.

## Standstill throws

**What:** throw from a sideways stance with no run-up at all. Feet stay
planted throughout; all power comes from hip rotation and weight shift
between the two planted feet.

**Why:** isolates upper-body mechanics, weight shift, and hit timing.
Removes the variables of x-step cadence and plant timing so you can
confirm the motion is grooved before adding momentum.

**How:**
1. Stand sideways to target, plant (lead) foot forward, rear foot back
2. Load weight onto the rear foot
3. Reach back along the throwing line (short, straight)
4. Initiate the throw by shifting weight forward onto the plant foot
5. Hips fire â†’ torso â†’ arm â†’ disc
6. Release, letting the rear leg naturally kick up behind in
   follow-through

**Cue:** "Start slow, keep the disc close, lead with the elbow, clean
release."

**Target:** 20â€“30 reps per session before moving to x-step. A
standstill throw should still go 70â€“80% of your full-run-up distance
if mechanics are sound.

## One-step throws

**What:** a single step into the plant instead of a full x-step.

**Why:** bridges the standstill and the x-step. Introduces the "trigger"
role of the plant (putting weight on the plant foot initiates the pull)
without the complexity of multiple strides.

**How:** start with rear foot slightly forward of the plant foot. Step
backward briefly, then forward into the plant. Feel that the instant
the plant lands, the pull begins.

## Hershyzer / Wall drill

**What:** a slow, controlled drill done next to a wall. You load into a
squat on your rear leg, then drive your rear hip *forward into the wall*
from the instep of the rear foot, coming up into your bracing plant.

**Why:** teaches **hip-lead**, **weight shift**, and the feeling of
**bracing** the plant leg. Because it's done against a wall, your body
can't over-rotate or collapse forward; the wall enforces structure.

**How:**
1. Stand perpendicular to the wall, throwing shoulder nearest the wall
2. Load: squat down onto the rear leg, closing the hips away from the
   wall
3. Drive: push off the rear instep, driving the rear hip forward toward
   the wall
4. Your butt/hip should contact the wall first â€” not your elbow or
   shoulder
5. The lead leg firms up as the bracing plant

**Cue:** "the slower, the better." If you're moving fast, you're using
momentum to mask balance issues. A well-executed Hershyzer is nearly
tai-chi-slow.

## Towel Snap drill

**What:** hold a small towel by one corner and try to crack it forward
as if throwing a backhand, without a disc.

**Why:** develops **snap** and forces a compact pull path. A towel only
cracks loudly if it's whipped tight â€” which only happens if the "pull"
stays close to the body (no rounding). A wide, rounded pull produces no
crack.

**How:** 10 reps per set, 3 sets. Then pick up a disc and try to
replicate the same felt sensation.

**Cue:** listen for the crack. Silence = rounded. Loud crack = tight
pull + snap.

## Straight Reach-Back drill

**What:** from standstill, reach the disc *straight behind* you along the
throwing line â€” no curl, no drop â€” and pull it straight back along the
same line.

**Why:** grooves the correct pull path in isolation. Directly targets
rounding by making the straight line the only path.

**How:** practice slowly, without throwing, focusing only on the reach
and initial pull direction. Add the actual throw after the motion feels
automatic.

## Wall drill (close-the-chest variant)

**What:** stand close to a wall on your off-hand (non-throwing) side.
Practice reach-back and pull motions without the disc contacting the
wall.

**Why:** forces the arm to stay tight to the body during the pull. If
the elbow flares out wide (rounding), it hits the wall.

## Obstacle drill

**What:** place a disc or cone on the back-inside of the tee pad (back
and to the left for RHBH) and throw without hitting it.

**Why:** obstacle forces a straighter, more compact pull path â€” the arm
can't loop around the body without striking the obstacle.

## Elbow-to-Target cue

**What:** not a drill by itself â€” a cue applied during any drill or
throw. At reach-back, point your throwing-side *elbow* at the target
(not the hand, not the disc â€” the elbow). Pull by bringing that elbow
past your ribs first.

**Why:** gives the body a physical landmark that keeps the elbow leading
the motion, which prevents the arm from drifting wide.

## Feet-Together drill

**What:** throw from a stance with feet together (touching).

**Why:** removes momentum as a crutch; exposes balance issues. Can't
complete a decent throw from this stance unless upper-body mechanics are
clean.

**How:** 5â€“10 reps at 60% effort. Goal is staying upright through the
follow-through.
