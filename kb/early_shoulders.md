# Early Shoulders & The Kinetic Sequence

**Tags:** `early_shoulders`, `kinetic_sequence`, `hip_lead`, `timing`, `x_factor`

## The problem

"Early shoulders" is when the upper body rotates open toward the target
*before* the hips initiate the turn. Symptoms:

- Power loss (the sequence never builds tension between lower and upper
  body)
- Pulls tend to drag toward the off-target side (right for RHBH, because
  the rotation is over before release)
- Arm-dominant throws — once shoulders have opened, the arm has nothing
  to sequence off of, so it just muscles the disc through

## The proper kinetic sequence

A well-sequenced backhand fires **from the ground up**:

1. **Plant foot contacts** the ground — weight begins transfer
2. **Front hip and glutes fire** — the hip initiates rotation
3. **Torso rotates** as a consequence of hip rotation (not on its own)
4. **Shoulders unwind** as the torso rotates through
5. **Elbow, grip, and disc** follow, with the snap at the end

The image to hold is a spring uncoiling: the hip twists *against* the
still-closed upper body, storing energy, and then the upper body releases
that stored energy through the chain.

## The "stretch reflex" / separation

The gap between hip angle and shoulder angle at the moment of peak
separation is sometimes called **"X-factor"** (borrowed from traditional
golf). More separation (hips further rotated than shoulders, briefly) =
more stored energy available to release. Pros typically have 30–45° of
separation at peak.

Early shoulders eliminate this separation — there's no gap because both
open together.

## Fixes

### Cue: "hip first"

Before every throw during practice, consciously cue the sequence: *plant,
hip fires, then everything else*. Amateur throwers often don't feel the
hip initiating because it's typically dormant.

### Drill: One-step throws

Start in a sideways stance with your throwing-side foot behind. Take ONE
step forward (the plant step only — no x-step), and feel that putting
weight onto the plant foot *initiates* the pull. If the pull starts with
your arm/shoulder instead, you know the timing is off.

### Drill: Hershyzer / Wall drill

See drills.md. The Hershyzer teaches hip-lead and weight-shift sequencing
by having you drive your back hip forward into a wall from a loaded,
squatted position. Slow it down — the slower you can do it with good
balance, the more the sequence grooves in.

### Cue: "close the door"

During reach-back, imagine a door closing behind you with your back. Your
back stays turned to the target (chest away) for a beat longer than feels
natural. When you pull, the hip opens the door first; the shoulders
follow.

## Why side view only partially shows this

From the side view, hip and shoulder rotation angles are *projected*
onto the camera plane, so the absolute magnitudes are distorted. What
**can** be read reliably from side view is the **timing**: at what moment
does the shoulder line start rotating vs. the hip line? A side camera
can reveal mis-sequencing even when it can't measure the exact angles.

Down-the-line gives the clearer view of hip-vs-shoulder separation
magnitude.
