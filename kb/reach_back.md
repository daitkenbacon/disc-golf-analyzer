# Reach-Back

**Tags:** `reach_back`, `timing`, `loading`

## Purpose

Reach-back is the *loading* phase — it puts the disc behind the body so
there is runway to accelerate into the pull. It is **not** a power source
in itself; it's a positioning step.

## Timing: the most important rule

**The peak of your reach-back should coincide with the moment your plant
foot hits the ground.** When plant contact and reach-back peak align, the
energy from the weight shift has somewhere to go — into the pull. When
they're mis-timed, you either:

- plant too early → the body starts pulling before the arm is loaded, so
  the arm plays catch-up (forces an arm-throw) OR
- plant too late → you've already started opening from the top while your
  foot is still in the air (early shoulders, no brace)

## Depth: less than you think

A pervasive myth in disc golf is that "deeper reach-back = more power." It
doesn't. Top backhand throwers barely reach back at all. What they do is:

- Extend the throwing arm **straight behind** them (along the throwing
  line), **not curled around the body**
- Keep the reach-back **compact and on the pull line**, not overextended

Over-reaching past the line of the throw causes the arm to drift wide and
makes rounding almost inevitable. A shorter, straighter reach-back is
preferred over a deeper, curled one.

## The line: across the chest, not around it

From reach-back, the disc should travel **on a line that runs across your
chest** — think of a cable strung from the reach-back point to the
release point, passing close to your sternum. The disc follows that
cable. If the reach-back is off-line (disc dipped below shoulder, arm
curled inward, etc.), the pull will also be off-line.

## The pull-through geometry

During the pull-through, **the elbow leads** while the disc stays tight to
the torso, then the elbow snaps forward at the very end. This is what
produces the "lawnmower cord" feel — a short pull with a quick snap,
rather than a long arcing push.

## Side-view vs. down-the-line read

- **Side view:** can measure reach-back *depth* (wrist position behind
  torso center) and timing relative to plant. Cannot see rounding directly.
- **Down-the-line:** can see whether the reach-back stayed on the
  throwing line or curled around the body. DTL is the better angle for
  diagnosing reach-back path issues.
