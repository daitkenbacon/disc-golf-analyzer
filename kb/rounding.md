# Rounding

**Tags:** `rounding`, `pull_through`, `power_leak`, `accuracy`

## What it is

Rounding occurs when, during the reach-back and pull-through, the arm curls
around the body instead of pulling the disc in a straight line across the
chest. The disc's path deviates from the throwing line into a horizontal arc.

Widely described as disc golf's #1 form flaw at amateur level. Costs both
distance (power leaks out through the wide arc) and accuracy (release timing
varies).

## Root cause

The throwing arm is starting the motion instead of responding to it. In a
proper backhand, the body turn leads and the arm extends as a downstream
consequence. When the arm initiates, the elbow drifts out wide of the torso
and the disc follows an arc rather than a straight pull line.

## The cue that fixes it

**Lead with the elbow; pull the disc on a straight line tight across the
chest.** The motion should feel like pulling a lawnmower cord — short,
straight, and close to the body. The elbow should brush near the ribs as the
disc travels past.

## Drills (see drills.md for full descriptions)

- **Straight Reach-Back drill** — groove the straight pull path in isolation
- **Wall drill / Hershyzer** — the butt hits the wall first instead of the
  elbow or shoulder, forcing the disc to stay close
- **Towel Snap drill** — wrap a towel around your fist; the towel only
  cracks if it stays tight against the chest through the pull
- **Obstacle drill** — place a disc behind you to the left (for RHBH) on
  the tee pad; throw without hitting it, forcing a straighter path
- **Field work with standstills + one-step** before adding the x-step

## Why it shows up in video

From the **side view:** rounding manifests as an elongated pull duration
(reach-back → hit > 400 ms) and a visible sagittal deviation of the wrist
from the straight reach-back → release line. Side view is an indirect read
— rounding is fundamentally a horizontal-plane arc.

From **down-the-line (behind thrower, facing target):** the arc is directly
visible as the disc path swings around the body rather than pulling tight
across the chest. DTL is the definitive angle for diagnosing rounding.

## Practice strategy

Repetition. The only way to remove a grooved bad habit is to groove the
correct motion thousands of times, starting with standstills and progressing
to full x-step only after the straight pull feels natural.
