# Snap, Hit Point, and the Power Pocket

**Tags:** `snap`, `hit_point`, `power_pocket`, `release`, `distance`, `spin`

## The hit point

The **hit point** is the instant in the throw where all the power generated
by the reach-back and pull-through is released — the moment you "hit"
forward and the disc leaves your hand. Everything before the hit is energy
accumulation; the hit is its transfer.

## The power pocket

The **power pocket** is the geometric position the arm must pass through
for maximum energy transfer:

- **Shoulder-to-bicep: ~90°**
- **Bicep-to-forearm: ~90°**
- The elbow is held *well away from the chest* at this position
- From this position, the arm functions as a set of levers, with the body's
  rotational energy amplified into the disc

If you never reach the power pocket — for example if the arm is still
straight when the body has already rotated past square — the lever action
never engages and the throw becomes an arm push.

## The snap

**Snap** is the rapid extension of the elbow at the hit, produced by the
arm being *left relaxed* while the body decelerates. The body's stop
(via the plant/brace) propagates up the chain; the relaxed elbow, unable
to stop as quickly, snaps into extension, sending rotational velocity into
the disc. That velocity becomes **spin**, which is what holds a disc on
line and gives it distance.

## Why a throw feels like it "slips out" with "no snap"

Three overlapping causes, all pointing at the same structural failure:

1. **No power pocket / rounding.** The arm never achieved the 90/90
   geometry so there was no lever to amplify. The arm pushed the disc
   instead of whipping it.
2. **Timing mismatch.** The body ran out of rotation before the arm caught
   up, so the "stop" that creates snap happened too early and the disc
   released passively.
3. **Grip too loose, or gripped without intent.** Without a firm grip until
   the instant of release, the fingers can't hold long enough for the arm
   extension to generate spin; the disc slides out.

All three are downstream symptoms of the same pattern: arm-dominant throw
with rounding. Fix the pull path (see rounding.md) and the snap usually
returns.

## Weight shift and bracing

The arm's snap only works if the body has produced force for it to
transfer. That force comes from the **weight shift from back foot to front
foot**, timed to arrive at the moment the disc passes the body mid-throw.
See plant_xstep.md for the bracing mechanics.

## Practical targets

- **Reach-back → hit duration:** 200–300 ms for efficient amateurs; pros
  faster. >400 ms = pull is too long and there's a rounding/arm-throw
  problem.
- **Felt sensation:** the hit should feel like the disc was *taken* from
  you, not released. If you're aware of opening your fingers, it's
  already too late.
